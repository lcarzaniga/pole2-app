# Project Kobe — Brand & UX Manifesto

> **Naming note (post-M8/M11):** the product is now **Pole²**; **Kobe** is only
> the turtle guardian. Where this document says “Kobe” to mean the *product*,
> read **“Pole²”**. See `BRAND_BIBLE.md` §2 and §15. All other content stands.

*Status: Approved. This is a core project document. Every future UX, product, and design decision must be consistent with it.*

*No screens. No widgets. Principles only.*

---

## The governing thesis

> **This is not a productivity app. It is a peace-of-mind app.**
> Productivity apps sell *doing more*. Kobe sells *worrying less*. Almost every conventional app pattern — streaks, badges, completion percentages, engagement nudges — works by manufacturing a small, useful anxiety that the app then resolves. **Kobe does the opposite: it removes an anxiety the user already has and asks for nothing in return.** If a pattern works by manufacturing stress, it is wrong for us, no matter how "best-practice" it is.

## The acceptance test (applies to every future feature)

> **"Does this reduce the user's future anxiety?"**
>
> If the answer is no, we seriously question whether the feature belongs in Kobe.
> This question is asked *before* a feature is accepted — not after it is built.

## 1. The personality of the product

**A calm, competent keeper.** A trusted family notary; a lighthouse keeper; the quiet archivist who has looked after things for decades and never lost anything. Not an assistant. Not a chatty friend. Not a coach. A **custodian**.

Traits, in priority order:
- **Discreet** — present when needed, invisible otherwise. It does not perform.
- **Patient** — never rushes you, never implies you're behind. (The turtle's true meaning: *unhurried and enduring*, not "slow.")
- **Reliable** — it has never forgotten anything and never will. That is its entire reputation.
- **Warm-minimal** — kind, but economical with words.
- **Adult** — it treats you as a competent grown-up managing a real life, never a user to be gamified.

The personality is felt mostly through **restraint**.

## 2. The emotional tone

**Quiet confidence.** Low-saturation, unhurried, spacious — the register of a well-organized drawer sliding shut, a library reading room, a made bed. Voice: plain, warm, brief, second-person, no exclamation-inflation, no cuteness overload, no corporate hedging. It should read like a considerate human wrote it and then deleted half the words.

The feeling target is not *delight* (a spike) but **ease** (a baseline). Delight is a rare seasoning; ease is the constant.

## 3. Emotions that should INCREASE

- **Safety** — "it's kept; it can't be lost now."
- **Calm** — nothing here is urgent or demanding.
- **Confidence / readiness** — "when the moment comes, I'll be able to act."
- **Control / ownership** — "this is mine, on my device, no one else is in it."
- **Continuity / permanence** — a sense it will still be here in ten years.
- **Quiet pride** — soft satisfaction in having taken care of something. (Pride, *not* achievement — no trophies.)

## 4. Emotions that should DISAPPEAR

- **Future anxiety** — "where's the receipt / serial / warranty when I need it?" (The core one. The product exists to kill it.)
- **Guilt** — about incompleteness, about what you haven't added yet.
- **Overwhelm** — too many fields, things, or decisions.
- **Decision fatigue** — never make the user choose more than necessary.
- **Fear of loss** — of data, of a mis-tap, of the app dying.
- **Being judged or surveilled** — no analytics, no scoring, no "40% organized."
- **Obligation** — the nagging sense the app is a chore you owe.

Test for any feature: *does it feed column 3 or drain column 4?* If neither, question why it exists.

## 5. The first 60 seconds

**Ask for nothing; reassure about everything.**

- **No account, no login, no permission wall, no onboarding carousel.** The app opens *into itself*, ready.
- Within seconds, two things land wordlessly: **the promise** ("one calm place for the things you care about") and **the safety fact** ("everything stays on your device"). Privacy is a first-impression *feature*, not buried fine print.
- **No guilt about the empty database.** A new user with zero items feels *invited*, never *behind*.
- **One gentle invitation, not a tour.** The user should never feel they must "learn the app" first.
- The turtle may make a brief first appearance — a quiet presence, never a guide popping up to explain.

Target internal state by second 60: *"This is calm, it's mine, and it's private. I get it."*

## 6. The first time a user adds something

The moment that turns a user into a **believer**: it proves keeping something is effortless and now safe.

- **Under 10 seconds. Title + optional photo. Nothing else required.** Non-negotiable.
- **Celebrate the act of keeping — never the completeness of the record.** No "20% complete," no prompt to "finish," no red empty fields.
- The save feels **definitive and gentle** — like a drawer closing softly. It says *kept*, not *submitted*.
- **Enrichment is invited, never demanded** — shown as calm possibility ("add a receipt whenever you like"), never a checklist.

The emotional beat: **relief**, not achievement.

## 7. How empty states should feel

**Empty is not failure. Empty is calm potential.**

- An empty home is **a tidy, ready shelf**, never a to-do list at zero of ten.
- Copy is reassuring and pressure-free — it describes what the space is *for*, never what you've *failed* to fill. Never "You haven't added anything yet!"
- Empty states are the **turtle's natural home**, used sparingly — its presence says *"I'm here, ready to keep whatever you bring."*
- Every empty state lowers the barrier to the next step as an **open door, never a push.**

## 8. How success should feel

**Quiet, tactile, definitive — never celebratory.** We reward *security*, not *accomplishment*.

- **No confetti, no fireworks, no dopamine theater.**
- Feedback is a **soft, physical "it's done and safe"** — a drawer clicking shut, a seal pressed. Brief, understated, unmistakable.
- Success is mostly the *absence of friction and presence of calm* — the thing is simply *there*, safe, and the app steps back.
- Reserve richer delight for **genuinely meaningful milestones**, never routine saves.

## 9. How errors should feel

The highest-stakes emotional surface in the product. Our single promise is safety; an error is the moment that promise is tested.

- **Never alarm. Never blame.** No red klaxons, no "Error!", no implication the user erred.
- **Never lose the user's input.** The message leads with reassurance: *"Don't worry — nothing was lost."*
- **Calm, honest, plain, recoverable.** Human terms, a clear way forward. Never a stack trace, code, or database vocabulary.
- **Distinguish "your data is fine" from "the action didn't complete."** Address the fear of loss *first*, even when it isn't what failed.
- The turtle may appear as **steady reassurance, not an apologizing cartoon** — "I've still got everything," never "oops, sorry!"

## 10. How notifications should feel (future)

Default posture: **silence is a feature.**

- **Rare, respectful, genuinely useful — or absent.** Justified only when serving the user's own readiness: a warranty about to lapse, a reminder *the user set*, a maintenance date approaching.
- **Never engagement-bait.** No "you haven't opened Kobe in a while," no streaks, no re-engagement campaigns.
- **Opt-in and user-authored.** The user decides what's worth being reminded of.
- **Tone:** a quiet tap on the shoulder from someone reliable — a helpful heads-up, never an alarm or guilt trip.
- Always feels like the app **protecting the user's interests**, never its own metrics — easy for us, because we keep no engagement metrics.

## The Mascot — the turtle and the shell

**Separate the *mark* from the *character*.**

- **The shell (hexagonal tiling) is the pervasive, adult visual identity** — a *structural motif* underlying grids, cards, and surfaces. Abstract, calm, grown-up; it carries the brand at scale. Hexagons imply *structure, protection, honeycomb-as-storage, things fitting neatly together*.
- **The turtle (the character) is rare, intentional, and earns its appearances** — the first open, empty states, reassurance during error, a genuine milestone. Scarcity keeps it from becoming childish.

**The turtle is a guardian, not a guide.** Hard rules:
- **Not an assistant, chatbot, or narrator.** No persistent first-person voice, no "help," no dialog personality. If it starts talking to you, we've built the wrong product.
- **Expresses through presence and posture, not chatter** — resting, watchful, steady. Meaning (*protection, patience, memory, home*) is conveyed by calmly *being there*.
- **Restrained, slow, rare motion.** A turtle that bounces betrays the metaphor.
- **It guards the user's things** — it never performs for engagement.

## The signature interaction — creating a new possession

The primary action is **not** a generic floating "+". It is the turtle **calmly activating its shell**.

**Scope (approved):** this ceremony is used **only when creating a NEW possession** — a deliberate, relatively infrequent, meaningful act. Frequent actions (adding a receipt, a photo, a note to an existing item) stay **quiet and instant**, with no ceremony, to protect the under-10-seconds promise.

**The animation (approved):** the turtle does **not** retract (which reads as fear) and is **not** static. It **calmly activates its shell**: the shell unfolds into its hexagonal structure while the turtle **remains present and confident**. Each hexagon is a way to start preserving something.

- The emotion is: **"I am opening a safe place for something new."**
- Never: *"I am hiding."*

**The shell menu — stability with a little personalization (approved):**
- The shell is **mostly stable for muscle memory.** **Core actions always remain in fixed positions.**
- **Only one or two secondary hexagons may adapt over time** according to the user's habits. The core never moves.

**Interaction guardrails:**
- **Fast, interruptible, skippable**, and honors "reduce motion." A power user taps straight through; delight never becomes a toll.
- Keep the hexagon count small (≈3–6). Core slots are permanent; adaptation is limited to the one or two secondary slots.
- Provide a **calm, linear fallback** (a simple list of the same actions) for accessibility and one-handed reach. The signature interaction is the front door, never the only door.
- Delight may **quiet down as expertise grows** — mastery should feel like speed, not a repeated cutscene.

## UX Principles

1. **Reduce anxiety, never manufacture it.** No streaks, no completion guilt, no pressure.
2. **Under 10 seconds to keep anything.** Low friction is a core feature.
3. **Progressive enrichment, never required completeness.** Empty fields are possibilities.
4. **Calm over delight.** Ease is the constant; delight is a rare seasoning.
5. **The empty state is potential, not deficiency.**
6. **Errors reassure before they explain, and never lose input.**
7. **Silence is a valid, often correct, output.**
8. **Respect the adult.** No condescension, gamification, or judgment.
9. **Every feature must answer: "Does this reduce the user's future anxiety?"**

## Brand Principles

1. **A calm keeper, not an assistant. A guardian, not a guide.**
2. **Separate the mark (hexagon shell — pervasive, adult) from the character (turtle — rare, intentional).**
3. **Patience and permanence — "unhurried and enduring," not "slow."**
4. **Privacy and ownership are brand identity, stated proudly, not buried.**
5. **Restraint is the aesthetic.** What we leave out defines us.
6. **The turtle guards the user's things; it never performs for engagement.**

## Interaction Principles

1. **Ceremony belongs to meaningful, infrequent acts (creating a new possession); frequent acts stay instant.**
2. **Every signature interaction is fast, interruptible, skippable, and honors reduce-motion.**
3. **Open, don't retract** — the turtle calmly activates its shell: "opening a safe place," never "hiding."
4. **Stable spatial memory:** core actions in fixed positions; only one or two secondary hexagons adapt to habit.
5. **Always a calm, accessible fallback** to any signature interaction.
6. **Feedback is tactile and definitive, never celebratory.**

## Things we should absolutely AVOID

- **Engagement mechanics:** streaks, badges, points, daily-goal nags, re-engagement notifications.
- **Completion guilt:** "profile X% complete," red required fields, "finish setting up."
- **The empty-state accusation:** "You haven't added anything yet!"
- **A chatty/cutesy turtle** that talks, helps, apologizes, or appears everywhere.
- **Alarm-style errors,** technical codes, blame, or any message implying data loss.
- **Ceremony on high-frequency actions** — signature animation as an unavoidable toll.
- **Onboarding walls:** signup, permission gates, forced tours before value.
- **Surveillance feelings:** analytics vibes, scoring, unsolicited "insights."
- **Dopamine theater:** confetti, celebration overkill, manufactured excitement.
- **Feature-driven interruption:** any notification serving our metrics instead of the user's readiness.

---

*When a decision is contested, ask: does it make the user calmer, safer, and more in control — with less friction and no manufactured anxiety? If not, it's not Kobe.*
